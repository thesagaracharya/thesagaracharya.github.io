A.01 Profile

Engineering judgment, backed by evidence.

I am a civil engineer based in Nepal, registered with the Nepal Engineering Council and the founder of Aakar Engineering Consultancy. My work sits where structural design meets project delivery: I produce structural designs, bills of quantities and cost estimates, plan and schedule construction, and assess the condition of existing structures.

My research interest is making infrastructure decisions defensible. In post-disaster contexts where destructive testing isn’t possible, I’ve developed integrated non-destructive testing frameworks that turn field readings into clear retain–repair–replace decisions. In project delivery, I study where intended schedules and standard time–cost models diverge, and how procurement rules shape environmental outcomes.

I’m currently deepening my toolkit in QGIS, Python data science, and machine learning — applying them to infrastructure asset management, project controls, and the digital-twin direction the field is heading.

01Research · NDT

Residual Strength Assessment of Fire-Exposed Buildings

Ongoing · Sole author

Problem. After fire damage, buildings must be judged safe, repairable, or demolished — but destructive coring is often impossible on politically sensitive or unstable sites. Approach. An integrated, NDT-only framework combining UPV, rebound/SonReb, impact-echo, infrared thermography, GPR, half-cell potential and portable hardness, normalised to on-site reference zones and codified reduction factors. Outcome. A replicable retain–repair–replace decision matrix tied to demand ratios, validated against Eurocode and ACI fire-degradation models, with full calculation sheets for each method.

06Road Infrastructure · DED

DED Cost Estimate — Campus Marga Road, Tilottama Municipality

Delivered

Project. Detailed Engineering Design (DED) cost estimation for Campus Marga Road Infrastructure, a 1.225 km urban road corridor (chainage 0+000 to 1+224.181) in Ward No. 16, Tilottama Municipality, Rupandehi — procured under RFP No. DUDBC/FPIU/RUPANDEHI/RFP/01/080-81 through the Integrated Urban Infrastructure Development Program (IUIDP). Client. Department of Urban Development and Building Construction (DUDBC), Federal Project Implementation Unit, Rupandehi, Government of Nepal. Scope. As cost estimator, prepared the full pre-construction cost estimate covering 12 major work categories: earthworks, concrete structures, bituminous pavements, drainage, road furniture, and bio-engineering works. Computed earthwork volumes using the cross-sectional area method across 63+ chainage stations derived from the detailed layout data. Estimated quantities for all pavement layers (sub-base, base course, asphalt concrete), covered RCC drains, kerb stones, interlocking block footpaths, and 62 street-lighting poles. All unit rates for labour, materials, and equipment benchmarked against DoR Standard Specifications and DOR Norms (FY 2080/81). Final Summary of Cost Estimate: approximately NPR 9.47 crore inclusive of VAT and contingencies.

Geometric Design. The horizontal alignment was defined by 76 Intersection Points (IPs) with simple circular curves — no spiral transition curves — and curve radii ranging from 15 m at the tightest urban bends (IPs 22–24, 27–29) to 941 m at the gentlest sweeps (IP 36), reflecting varied urban geometry along the corridor. The vertical profile traverses extremely flat Terai terrain, with elevations rising gently from 117.47 m to 120.02 m — a net gain of roughly 2.35 m over 1.225 km. Of the 15 vertical intersection points, only four carry a formed vertical curve (each 20 m long, at VIPs 5, 9, 13, and 14); the remainder are tangent grade changes, consistent with the near-level longitudinal profile.

Cross-Section & Pavement Composition. The standard urban cross-section carries a 3.55 m carriageway on each side (two-lane configuration), flanked by a 0.2 m covered RCC drain and a 0.3 m footpath on both left and right. A normal cross-fall of 2.5% was applied on tangent sections, transitioning to superelevated sections at curves — reaching up to 6% on tighter bends and up to 5.5% (adverse-side superelevation) at IP 73 — with extra carriageway widening applied at sharper horizontal curves to maintain safe vehicle tracking. Pavement layers follow DoR specifications for urban bituminous construction: granular sub-base, wet mix macadam base course, and asphalt concrete wearing course.

Drainage & Road Furniture. Covered RCC drains run continuously along both edges of the carriageway, sized to manage the urban stormwater load in the flat Terai catchment. Road furniture quantities include kerb stones delineating the carriageway–footpath interface, interlocking concrete block paving on footpaths for pedestrian access, and 62 street-lighting poles spaced along the 1.225 km corridor to meet urban illumination standards. Coordinate and design-level data at every chainage station — referenced to UTM Easting/Northing and finished road level — provided the geometric control for all drainage invert and furniture-setting calculations.
