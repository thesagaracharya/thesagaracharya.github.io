A Self-Calibrating, Cross-Code NDT Framework for Residual-Strength Assessment of Fire-Exposed Structures

Thesis research proposal · sole author · July 2026 · field deployment without coring, validated against laboratory ground truth · full proposal (PDF)

Problem
When a building survives a serious fire, it hides its own damage: concrete can look sound while its core has lost half its strength; steel can stand plumb while its yield capacity has quietly collapsed. The retain–repair–replace decision is one of the highest-stakes judgments in structural engineering, yet visual inspection reads only the surface, and destructive coring — the supposed gold standard — is frequently unsafe, unaffordable, or not permitted on unstable, occupied, heritage, or access-restricted sites. Engineers are forced to choose between a method that is unreliable and one that is unavailable.
Approach
A self-calibrating, cross-code, NDT-only framework. The surviving structure becomes its own calibration standard: every SonReb and hardness correlation is anchored to a minimally-affected reference zone within the same building through a calibration coefficient β, so the largest sources of NDT bias divide out without removing any material. Every element’s residual capacity is then computed twice — under Eurocode 2/3 and under ACI 228/216 — and the divergence between the codes is reported as data, with the governing (lower) result driving the decision. Field deployment is core-free, but the method is validated against genuine ground truth before field use.
Methods
An eight-stage pipeline (Stage 0–7): criteria-based case selection; visual triage (D0–D3) with quantitative reference-zone certification; two parallel NDT suites (UPV, rebound/SonReb, impact-echo, infrared thermography, GPR, half-cell + resistivity for concrete; portable hardness, ultrasonic thickness, MT/DP, and geometric survey for steel); formally defined self-calibration; uncertainty via first-order error propagation and 10,000-draw Monte Carlo simulation with sensitivity analysis, carrying the 5th-percentile lower bound into design; dual-code capacity computation; and a governing demand ratio. A companion laboratory series — specimens cast, heated to controlled temperatures (20–900 °C), tested non-destructively, then crushed — validates the calibrated predictor (R², RMSE, bias, Bland–Altman limits) and benchmarks it against published fire-test data.
Results
Expected outputs: a validated, field-implementable retain–repair–replace decision matrix (Retain: DR ≥ 1.00 · Repair: 0.70 ≤ DR < 1.00 · Replace: DR < 0.70) with an auditable chain from measurement to decision; a first quantified, element-by-element comparison of Eurocode and ACI residual-capacity outcomes on real fire-damaged members; and a rare matched NDT–true-strength dataset. Planned scope: 4–6 fire-damaged case structures (60–120 elements, ≥2 methods each) and ≥30 lab specimens; buildings damaged in the 2025 Nepal unrest form the initial validation set, with selection criteria defined independently of any single site.
Impact
Converts a subjective, high-stakes judgment into an evidence-based, auditable one: avoided over-demolition of salvageable structure, prevention of catastrophic under-assessment, faster reoccupation, and better-targeted repair spend — serving owners, engineers, insurers, regulators, and reconstruction agencies. Proposed as a 12-month thesis; I am seeking faculty supervision, particularly on the four make-or-break challenges the proposal treats explicitly (see appendix).
Technical appendix — pipeline, calibration, thresholds & sampling

Eight-stage pipeline

  • Stage 0 — criteria-based case selection against pre-registered criteria (documented exposure, representative construction type, internal reference zone, safe access, design drawings)
  • Stage 1 — visual triage into damage classes D0–D3 and reference-zone certification by diagnostic thresholds, not visual judgment (surface colorimetry for the >300 °C pink/grey transition, resistivity/half-cell dry-baseline band, UPV + impact-echo soundness)
  • Stage 2 — integrated NDT collection: every element characterized by at least two independent methods (ASTM C597, C805, C1383, C876; single-method results flagged provisional and reduced 15%)
  • Stage 3 — self-calibration: site-corrected readings normalized to the reference zone (V̄ = Vcorr/Vref, R̄ = Rcorr/Rref); β = f′c,ref / SonReb(Vref, Rref) forces the model to reproduce the known reference strength; identical logic maps steel hardness to residual yield
  • Stage 3a — validation against laboratory ground truth and literature benchmarks (Eurocode 2 / ACI-NIST residual factors); only correlations passing both checks proceed to field use
  • Stage 4 — residual properties as distributions: error propagation + Monte Carlo; design value = 5th percentile ≈ f̂′c,res − 1.645 sf
  • Stage 5–6 — dual-code capacity (EN 1992-1-2 / 1993-1-2 vs ACI 228 / 216) on identical inputs; divergence tabulated element by element; governing demand ratio DR = φRn/U
  • Stage 7 — classification with justified thresholds, plus a global ambient-vibration cross-check (measured/estimated first-mode frequency < 0.85 triggers denser testing and a capacity downgrade)

Four make-or-break challenges (supervision sought)

  • Reference-zone certification — fixing the quantitative thresholds (temperature ceiling, resistivity band) that admit a zone as a genuine baseline; near 150 °C, moisture evaporation raises UPV without a matching strength loss
  • Cover vs core under steep gradients — the rebound hammer reads the degraded outer ~30 mm while direct-transmission UPV is dominated by the sound core; treated with impact-echo depth profiling, surface-wave UPV, and thermocouple-instrumented lab gradients
  • Steel hardness vs residual yield — dual mapping of HV against both fy and fu, across air-cooled and water-quenched regimes and stratified by steel grade; doubtful connection members routed to Replace regardless of hardness
  • Lab-to-field restraint & scale transfer — an explicit, one-sided model-error term (may under-predict field capacity, constrained not to over-predict it) injected into the uncertainty budget, cross-checked by ambient vibration

Fallbacks

  • Reference zone fire-affected or absent → hierarchy of external anchors (unheated upper-floor element, protected core, design + mill certificates), else the lab-calibrated correlation with temperature reconstruction and widened uncertainty bounds
  • Unsafe-to-access members conservatively assigned the worst class of their group pending later inspection

15-Bedded Primary Hospital, Nuwakot — Full Project-Controls Package

Multi-storey (G+3 + ramp block) RCC primary hospital on a 1,924 m² site

Problem
A new primary hospital needed the complete pre-construction planning, estimation, and controls package before works could be procured and delivered.
Approach
Built the package end to end: detailed quantity estimate, rate analysis, cost abstract, a resource-loaded master schedule, and inventory classification, closed out with WBS, environmental management, and health & safety plans.
Methods
48-page element-by-element measurement with door/window deductions; rate analysis for 60 work items built up from labour, material, and equipment per district rates (FY 2081/82); a 277-activity resource-loaded schedule in MS Project; ABC inventory classification on the materials basket.

Detailed Engineering Design (DED) cost estimation for an urban road corridor procured under RFP No. DUDBC/FPIU/RUPANDEHI/RFP/01/080-81 through the Integrated Urban Infrastructure Development Program (IUIDP), for the Department of Urban Development and Building Construction (DUDBC), Federal Project Implementation Unit, Rupandehi, Government of Nepal.

As cost estimator, prepared the full pre-construction estimate across 12 major work categories: earthworks, concrete structures, bituminous pavements, drainage, road furniture, and bio-engineering works.

Earthwork volumes computed by the cross-sectional area method across 63+ chainage stations; quantities estimated for all pavement layers (sub-base, base course, asphalt concrete), covered RCC drains, kerb stones, interlocking block footpaths, and 62 street-lighting poles. Technical appendix — geometric design, cross-section & drainage

Geometric design

  • Horizontal alignment — defined by 76 Intersection Points (IPs) with simple circular curves (no spiral transitions); curve radii ranging from 15 m at the tightest urban bends (IPs 22–24, 27–29) to 941 m at the gentlest sweeps (IP 36), reflecting varied urban geometry along the corridor.
  • Vertical profile — extremely flat Terai terrain; elevations rise gently from 117.47 m to 120.02 m, a net gain of roughly 2.35 m over 1.225 km. Of 15 vertical intersection points, only four carry a formed vertical curve (each 20 m long, at VIPs 5, 9, 13, and 14); the remainder are tangent grade changes, consistent with the near-level longitudinal profile.

Cross-section & pavement composition

  • Standard urban cross-section: 3.55 m carriageway on each side (two-lane), flanked by a 0.2 m covered RCC drain and a 0.3 m footpath on both left and right.
  • Normal cross-fall of 2.5% on tangents, transitioning to superelevation at curves — up to 6% on tighter bends and up to 5.5% (adverse-side) at IP 73 — with extra carriageway widening at sharper horizontal curves to maintain safe vehicle tracking.
  • Pavement per DoR specifications for urban bituminous construction: granular sub-base, wet mix macadam base course, and asphalt concrete wearing course.

Drainage & road furniture

  • Covered RCC drains run continuously along both carriageway edges, sized for the urban stormwater load in the flat Terai catchment.
  • Kerb stones delineating the carriageway–footpath interface; interlocking concrete block paving on footpaths; 62 street-lighting poles spaced along the corridor to meet urban illumination standards.

Property & Fixed-Asset Valuation — 53+ Sites Across Nepal

Aakar Engineering Consultancy · land, buildings & fixed assets · NVA-trained (Associate VPO of IVSC)

Problem
Every lending, purchase, or compliance decision on a property ultimately rests on one number — its value — and that number is only defensible when it is built from verified facts on the ground, not from paper claims. Documents overstate, boundaries drift, and construction quality varies member by member.
Approach
End-to-end valuation of more than 53 sites across Nepal through Aakar Engineering Consultancy: detailed physical site inspection, document verification against ground reality, reasoned analysis of land and building value, and a signed report that a bank, buyer, or owner can rely on and audit.
Methods
On-site inspection with physical measurement of land and structures; cross-verification of land-ownership certificates (lalpurja), cadastral maps, and approved building drawings against what actually stands on the ground; boundary, access, road-frontage, and setback checks; assessment of building condition, construction quality, and remaining useful life; land valued from prevailing market evidence alongside government rates, structures by the cost approach with depreciation — concluding in fair market value and distress value for each asset.
Results
53+ completed valuation assignments spanning residential, semi-commercial, and mixed-use properties, each documented as an auditable chain from field measurement and photographic record to concluded value.
Impact
The same verification-first discipline that runs through my research, applied at scale in practice. Grounded in formal training: Theory and Practice of Fixed Assets Valuation (Nepal Valuer’s Association, Associate VPO of IVSC — intensive course, Jan 2025, Cert. VT3-28-592) and the Lalpurja Academy Real Estate Agent Masterclass (LARM).
